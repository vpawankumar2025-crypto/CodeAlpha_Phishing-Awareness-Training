🎣 What is Phishing?

Phishing is a social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate trusted entities to trick victims into revealing sensitive information — passwords, credit card numbers, or installing malware.

⚠️
Scale of the threat: Over 3.4 billion phishing emails are sent every single day. Phishing accounts for more than 90% of all data breaches worldwide. No organisation is immune.
🎯

Targeted

Attackers research their victims carefully to craft convincing, personalised messages.

⚡

Fast

Credentials can be harvested within minutes of a victim clicking a malicious link.

💰

Profitable

Phishing causes over $1.8 billion in losses annually, making it cybercrime's top earner.

🔄

Evolving

Attackers constantly update tactics to bypass security filters and fool trained users.

How a Phishing Attack Works

  1. Reconnaissance — Attacker researches the target (LinkedIn, company website, social media).
  2. Craft the lure — A convincing email/SMS/call is prepared with spoofed branding.
  3. Deliver — Message is sent to the victim via email, SMS, social media, or phone.
  4. Hook — Victim clicks the malicious link or attachment.
  5. Harvest — Credentials or malware payload are captured.
  6. Exploit — Attacker uses the stolen data for financial gain or further attacks.

🕵️ Types of Phishing Attacks

Not all phishing is the same. Here are the most common variants attackers use today.

📧

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating banks, services, or IT departments. The most common type — often generic but sent in massive volumes.

🎯

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted at a specific person using personal details. Much more convincing and harder to detect than bulk phishing.

🐋

Whaling

Targets high-level executives (CEO, CFO). Emails appear to come from boards or regulators, demanding urgent wire transfers.

📱

Smishing (SMS)

"Your package is held — click to release." Text messages with malicious links, exploiting mobile users' trust.

📞

Vishing (Voice)

Phone calls impersonating banks or tech support. Attackers use urgency and fear to extract information verbally.

🔍

Search Engine Phishing

Fake websites ranked in Google results for terms like "bank login". Victims land on clones that steal credentials.

🎭
Business Email Compromise (BEC): A sophisticated form of spear phishing where attackers compromise or impersonate a business email account to authorise fraudulent wire transfers. Average loss per incident exceeds $125,000.

Universal Red Flags to Remember

⏰

Urgency & Pressure

"Act now", "immediate action required", "24-hour deadline" — designed to stop you thinking.

🔗

Suspicious Links

Hover before clicking. Mismatched URLs, misspellings, or unexpected domains are red flags.

📎

Unexpected Attachments

Never open .exe, .zip, .doc with macros, or .pdf from unknown senders.

🔑

Asking for Credentials

Legitimate services never ask for your password via email.

✍️

Grammar & Spelling

Poor writing, strange punctuation, or odd phrasing often indicates foreign-language attackers.

🎭

Mismatched Branding

Wrong logo colours, outdated branding, or off fonts indicate a fake page.

🛡️ Best Practices — Your Defence Plan

Check off each practice as you adopt it. Building good habits is the strongest defence against phishing.

  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all important accounts — email, banking, social media.
  • Always hover over links before clicking to verify the actual destination URL.
  • Verify the sender's full email address — not just the display name.
  • Never enter credentials on a site that lacks HTTPS (look for the padlock icon).
  • Call organisations on their official number to verify any suspicious requests before acting.
  • Keep your OS, browser, and antivirus up to date to patch known vulnerabilities.
  • Use a password manager — it won't autofill credentials on fake sites.
  • Report phishing emails to your IT/security team and to the impersonated organisation.
  • Be extra cautious of requests for gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency payments.
  • Treat every unexpected email attachment as potentially malicious until verified.
💡
Golden Rule: When in doubt, don't click. Contact the supposed sender through a separately verified channel — look up their number or website yourself, never use contact details provided in the suspicious message.
